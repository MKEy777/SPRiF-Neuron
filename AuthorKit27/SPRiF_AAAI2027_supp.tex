\section{Supplementary Guide and Evidence Map}
\label{supp:sec:guide}

This technical appendix expands the evidence used in the main paper rather than introducing a second line of claims. It provides reproducibility details for the five benchmark results, exact definitions for the mechanism variants and SI-DMS intervention, and the formal SPRiF update plus dynamical-analysis protocols.

\paragraph{Consistency locks.}
All sections preserve the main-paper contract: the slow spectral state is three-dimensional, the fast discharge state is two-dimensional, and each timestep follows $I_t\to\mathbf{x}_t\to\widetilde{\mathbf{u}}_t\to v_t\to z_t\to\mathbf{u}_t$. Projective reset acts directly only on the fast discharge state, with $\Delta\mathbf{x}_t^{\mathrm{reset}}=\mathbf{0}$; in recurrent layers, spikes may still affect the next slow-state update through the ordinary $W_{\mathrm{rec}}\mathbf{z}_t$ input pathway. We use the main-paper terms \emph{functional state decomposition}, \emph{slow spectral state}, \emph{fast discharge state}, and \emph{projective reset} without redefining them. Benchmark SPRiF entries are five-seed mean$\pm$standard deviation, SI-DMS entries are three-seed summaries, and cross-paper comparator entries remain descriptive comparisons.

\paragraph{Evidence map.}
Each section below expands a specific main-paper element rather than introducing a second line of claims:
\begin{itemize}
\item Main-paper Table~1 is expanded by the dataset encodings, architectures, optimization settings, and reporting rules in Sections~\ref{supp:sec:protocols}--\ref{supp:sec:reporting}; the SPRiF entries remain five-seed estimates and published comparator entries remain descriptive context.
\item Main-paper Table~2 is expanded by the variant definitions in Section~\ref{supp:sec:variant-definitions}: A is $\omega=0$, B is the merged slow/fast state that changes both state role and total state dimension, and C is $\lambda=0$.
\item The SI-DMS results are expanded by the recurrent task construction, paired-loss definition, intervention-fraction sweep, delay-by-$K$ coverage, and integrity audit in Sections~\ref{supp:sec:sidms-protocol}--\ref{supp:sec:sidms-integrity}. Training uses $K\in\{0,1,2,4,8\}$ at a fixed 15\% intervention fraction; evaluation extends to $K\in\{16,32,40\}$ and fractions from 5\% to 50\%.
\item The S-MNIST loss-landscape diagnostic in Section~\ref{supp:sec:loss-landscape} compares one saved SPRiF checkpoint with one saved LIF checkpoint on a shared perturbation-coordinate grid. Because it uses one training seed and one random direction pair, it is treated as a checkpoint-level optimization diagnostic rather than a multi-seed generalization result.
\item The mechanism and temporal-kernel figures are expanded by the reset identities and impulse/spectral protocols in Sections~\ref{supp:sec:dynamics}--\ref{supp:sec:analysis-protocols}.
\item A fixed-checkpoint feature-perturbation diagnostic is reported separately in Section~\ref{supp:sec:perturbation-diagnostic}; it records how saved checkpoints respond under feature perturbations without adding a new benchmark, robustness ranking, or biological interpretation for $\lambda$.
\end{itemize}

\section{Experimental Protocols and Reporting}
\label{supp:sec:protocols}

The five benchmark entries use task-standard inputs and readouts. S-MNIST and PS-MNIST use MNIST~\cite{lecun1998gradient} tensors read as 784-step pixel sequences; PS-MNIST applies a seed-generated random pixel permutation fixed within the run. QTDB uses the public QT Database~\cite{laguna1997database} and follows the ASRNN encoding protocol~\cite{asrnn}: two normalized ECG channels are converted by increasing and decreasing level-crossing events into four input event streams, and a time-distributed classifier predicts six waveform classes at each timestep. The released training scripts consume the encoded \texttt{QTDB\_train.mat} and \texttt{QTDB\_test.mat} files; this appendix does not claim to provide a raw QTDB-to-\texttt{.mat} converter. GSC uses one-second utterances from Google Speech Commands~\cite{warden2018speech}, represented as 40-bin mel features with first- and second-order deltas stacked over 101 frames, followed by temporal mean pooling over hidden spikes. SHD uses the 700-channel cochlea event sequences from the Spiking Heidelberg Digits dataset~\cite{cramer2022heidelberg}, with recurrent hidden processing and accumulated readout evidence after warm-up.

\subsection{Seeds, Model Selection, and Reporting}
\label{supp:sec:reporting}

All five benchmarks use the seed set $\{0,1,2,42,1111\}$. For each benchmark and seed, the checkpoint is selected according to validation accuracy, and Table~1 reports the mean and standard deviation of the resulting evaluation accuracies over the five independently seeded runs. Within a task, all runs use the same task-specific preprocessing, data splits, and architecture settings in Table~\ref{tab:supp_arch}; only the seeded training realization changes. \subsection{Compute Environment}
\label{supp:sec:compute}

Experiments were run on one NVIDIA GeForce RTX 2080 Ti GPU with 11~GB of memory and 12 vCPUs of an Intel Xeon Platinum 8255C CPU at 2.50~GHz. \subsection{S-MNIST Loss-Landscape Diagnostic}
\label{supp:sec:loss-landscape}

We probe local parameter sensitivity around trained S-MNIST solutions using a two-dimensional random-plane visualization inspired by filter-normalized loss landscapes~\cite{li2018visualizing}. The SPRiF and LIF models use the same two recurrent layer widths, $[64,256]$, temporal-mean readout, normalized 784-step S-MNIST inputs, optimizer, learning-rate schedule, and seed. Their neuron dynamics differ, so the saved models contain 92,810 and 88,970 parameters, respectively; this is therefore an architecture-width-matched rather than parameter-count-matched comparison. The checkpoint metadata record test accuracies of 98.88\% for SPRiF and 95.19\% for LIF. These values identify the analyzed single-seed checkpoints and do not replace the five-seed benchmark summaries in the main paper.

For each checkpoint $\boldsymbol{\theta}^{\star}$, we generate two Gaussian random directions $\mathbf{d}_1$ and $\mathbf{d}_2$ using seeds 123 and 124. For every multidimensional parameter tensor $W$, its corresponding direction tensor $D$ is rescaled as $D\leftarrow D\lVert W\rVert_F/(\lVert D\rVert_F+10^{-10})$; one-dimensional parameters, including biases and neuron-wise dynamics parameters, are held fixed. We then evaluate
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}(a,b)=\mathcal{L}\!\left(\boldsymbol{\theta}^{\star}+a\mathbf{d}_1+b\mathbf{d}_2\right),
\qquad (a,b)\in[-1,1]^2,
\label{eq:supp_loss_landscape}
\end{equation}
on a $25\times25$ grid. Cross-entropy is estimated on the first four non-shuffled test mini-batches (2,048 images), with the 784-step sequence processed in chunks of 262 steps and the chunk-level logits averaged. Both models use the same coordinate range, grid resolution, and evaluation samples; the directions are normalized separately against each model's checkpoint tensors.

Figure~\ref{fig:supp_loss_landscape} shows a broader low-loss neighborhood for the analyzed SPRiF checkpoint. At the unperturbed center, the estimated cross-entropy is 0.0575 for SPRiF and 0.2098 for LIF. Over the 625 sampled coordinates, the SPRiF slice has lower loss at 85.1\% of points, with mean losses of 10.69 versus 15.78 and maxima of 36.46 versus 50.85. Thus, under these norm-matched perturbations, this SPRiF solution is less sensitive along the sampled plane. The result is deliberately scoped to one checkpoint and one direction pair: it does not characterize the full high-dimensional landscape, isolate which SPRiF component creates the geometry, or by itself establish a causal link between flatness and generalization.

\subsection{Model Controls and Integrity Audit}
\label{supp:sec:sidms-integrity}

Under the hard threshold rule in Eq.~\eqref{eq:supp_spike}, this yields an actual spike and then applies the model's ordinary reset rule. The intervention therefore does not bypass reset and does not add a separate post-spike update.

For SPRiF, let the reset direction be $\mathbf{r}=[1,\lambda_j]^\top$, so the fast-state reset identity is
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{u}_t-\bigl(\widetilde{\mathbf{u}}_t-z_t\theta\mathbf{r}\bigr)=\mathbf{0},
\label{eq:supp_reset_residual}
\end{equation}
while the slow state has no reset update,
\begin{equation}
\Delta\mathbf{x}_t^{\mathrm{reset}}=\mathbf{0}.
\label{eq:supp_slow_insulation}
\end{equation}
These are structural identities of the stated update. They formalize the SI-DMS distinction: forced spikes can alter the fast discharge state through real spike-and-reset events, but they do not directly modify the slow spectral state at that timestep.

\section{Formal SPRiF Specification and Analysis Protocols}
\label{supp:sec:dynamics}

\subsection{Slow Spectral State}
\label{supp:sec:slow-state}

The slow spectral state $\mathbf{x}_t\in\mathbb{R}^3$ evolves as
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{x}_t = A\mathbf{x}_{t-1} + B I_t,
\label{eq:supp_slow}
\end{equation}
where
\begin{equation}
A =
\begin{bmatrix}
\alpha & 0 & 0 \\
0 & \rho\cos\omega & -\rho\sin\omega \\
0 & \rho\sin\omega & \rho\cos\omega
\end{bmatrix}, \quad
B =
\begin{bmatrix}
1-\alpha \\
1-\rho \\
0
\end{bmatrix}.
\label{eq:supp_AB}
\end{equation}
The first coordinate is a real exponential trace,
\begin{equation}
x_t^0 = \alpha x_{t-1}^0 + (1-\alpha)I_t,
\label{eq:supp_real_mode}
\end{equation}
and the second and third coordinates form a damped rotational pair driven through the first oscillatory coordinate:
\begin{align}
x_t^1 &= \rho\cos\omega\, x_{t-1}^1 - \rho\sin\omega\, x_{t-1}^2 + (1-\rho)I_t, \\
x_t^2 &= \rho\sin\omega\, x_{t-1}^1 + \rho\cos\omega\, x_{t-1}^2.
\label{eq:supp_rot_mode}
\end{align}
The eigenvalues of $A$ are $\alpha$ and $\rho e^{\pm i\omega}$. We parameterize
\begin{equation}
\alpha=\sigma(\alpha_{\mathrm{raw}}),\quad
\rho=\sigma(\rho_{\mathrm{raw}}),\quad
\omega=\pi\sigma(\omega_{\mathrm{raw}}),
\label{eq:supp_param}
\end{equation}
so $\alpha,\rho\in(0,1)$ and $\omega\in(0,\pi)$, giving spectral radius $\max(\alpha,\rho)<1$. These sigmoid parameterizations keep the slow spectral state strictly stable under the learned raw variables.

Under a constant input $I_t=c$, the real mode converges to $c$. The rotational pair converges to the unique fixed point
\begin{align}
\mathbf{x}^{\mathrm{osc}}_\ast
&=
\bigl(I-\rho R_\omega\bigr)^{-1}
\begin{bmatrix}
(1-\rho)c \\
0
\end{bmatrix},
\label{eq:supp_osc_fixed_point}\\
R_\omega
&=
\begin{bmatrix}
\cos\omega & -\sin\omega \\
\sin\omega & \cos\omega
\end{bmatrix}.
\end{align}
Thus the first oscillatory coordinate is not generally equal to $c$ when $\omega\neq0$.

For a unit input impulse at $t=0$ with $\mathbf{x}_{-1}=\mathbf{0}$, the slow-state impulse response isolates the two retention modes separately:
\begin{align}
x_k^0 &= (1-\alpha)\alpha^k, \\
x_k^1 &= (1-\rho)\rho^k\cos(k\omega), \\
x_k^2 &= (1-\rho)\rho^k\sin(k\omega),
\end{align}
for $k\geq0$. The real mode provides monotone retention, whereas the rotational pair provides damped phase dynamics.

\subsection{Fast Discharge State and Projective Reset}
\label{supp:sec:fast-state}

The fast discharge state $\mathbf{u}_t\in\mathbb{R}^2$ receives a projection of the slow spectral state through $G\in\mathbb{R}^{2\times3}$:
\begin{align}
\widetilde{u}_t^0 &= \eta_0 u_{t-1}^0 + \kappa u_{t-1}^1 + (G\mathbf{x}_t)^0, \label{eq:supp_fast0} \\
\widetilde{u}_t^1 &= \eta_1 u_{t-1}^1 + (G\mathbf{x}_t)^1, \label{eq:supp_fast1}
\end{align}
with $\eta_0,\eta_1\in(0,1)$ parameterized as sigmoid-transformed raw variables. The membrane potential is $v_t=\widetilde{u}_t^0$, and the spike is
\begin{equation}
z_t = H(v_t-\theta),
\label{eq:supp_spike}
\end{equation}
where $H$ is the Heaviside step function.

Within each timestep, computation follows
$I_t=W_{\mathrm{in}}\mathbf{s}_t+W_{\mathrm{rec}}\mathbf{z}_{t-1}+b
\to \mathbf{x}_t=A\mathbf{x}_{t-1}+BI_t
\to \widetilde{\mathbf{u}}_t
\to v_t=\widetilde{u}_t^0
\to z_t=H(v_t-\theta)
\to \mathbf{u}_t=\widetilde{\mathbf{u}}_t-z_t\theta\mathbf{r}$.
This order matches the main paper and confines the local reset update to the fast discharge state.

Component-wise, projective reset is
\begin{align}
u_t^0 &= \widetilde{u}_t^0 - z_t\theta, \\
u_t^1 &= \widetilde{u}_t^1 - z_t\theta\lambda_j.
\end{align}
Setting $\lambda_j=0$ recovers scalar subtraction on the membrane coordinate; learned $\lambda_j$ allows reset to act along $[1,\lambda_j]^\top$ in the fast state.

\subsection{Surrogate Gradient}
\label{supp:sec:surrogate}

The forward pass uses the hard threshold in Eq.~\eqref{eq:supp_spike}. During backpropagation, the threshold derivative is replaced by the multi-Gaussian surrogate~\cite{asrnn}:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\frac{\partial z_t}{\partial v_t} \approx \gamma\big[&
a_0 \mathcal{N}(v_t-\theta;\,0,\sigma_0^2) \\
&{}-a_1\mathcal{N}(v_t-\theta;\,\mu_1,\sigma_1^2) \\
&{}-a_1\mathcal{N}(v_t-\theta;\,-\mu_1,\sigma_1^2)\big],
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:supp_surrogate}
\end{equation}
where $\sigma_0=0.5$, $a_0=1.15$, $\mu_1=0.5$, $\sigma_1=3.0$, $a_1=0.15$, and $\gamma=0.5$. The forward dynamics remain unchanged.

\subsection{Initialization, State Size, and Cost}
\label{supp:sec:initialization-cost}

The task-specific initialization ranges are listed in Table~\ref{tab:supp_arch}. For each decay $q\in\{\alpha,\rho,\eta_0,\eta_1\}$, a time constant $\tau_q$ in steps is mapped to $q=\exp(-1/\tau_q)$ before conversion to its unconstrained raw parameter. Raw variables are initialized so that the transformed $\alpha$, $\rho$, $\eta_i$, and $\omega$ values fall in the listed ranges; after initialization, optimization proceeds in the unconstrained raw parameter space.

Each SPRiF neuron stores five state values $(x^0,x^1,x^2,u^0,u^1)$ and has 13 neuron-wise dynamics parameters: $\alpha_{\mathrm{raw}}$, $\rho_{\mathrm{raw}}$, $\omega_{\mathrm{raw}}$, $\eta_{0,\mathrm{raw}}$, $\eta_{1,\mathrm{raw}}$, $\kappa$, the six entries of $G\in\mathbb{R}^{2\times3}$, and $\lambda$. The weight matrices $W_{\mathrm{in}}$ and $W_{\mathrm{rec}}$ and the bias $b$ follow the layer architecture.

For a layer of width $h$, the local state update cost is $O(h)$ because each neuron uses fixed-size slow and fast states. When recurrent connectivity is enabled, the $O(h^2)$ multiplication $W_{\mathrm{rec}}\mathbf{z}_{t-1}$ dominates for both LIF-style recurrent SNNs and SPRiF layers.

\subsection{Analysis Protocols}
\label{supp:sec:analysis-protocols}

For the impulse-response and temporal-kernel analyses in the main paper, we inject a unit impulse into trained SPRiF neurons and then record 100 steps of intrinsic slow-state evolution without further input. The four displayed SPRiF trajectories use the 10th, 40th, 70th, and 90th percentiles of learned $\alpha$. For the GSC and QTDB cross-model panels, the 90th-percentile SPRiF real mode is paired with the available trained ASRNN membrane kernel having the closest derived $\tau_m$, and each displayed kernel is normalized by its peak magnitude. Spectra from the real coordinate are aggregated across neurons. Because no subsequent input is applied and the slow state has no direct local reset term, the observed response shapes reflect the learned spectral parameters rather than later post-spike correction.

The learned reset-direction analysis uses the $\lambda$ values shown in the main-paper mechanism figure. Across QTDB, GSC, and PS-MNIST, the reported $\lambda$ distributions span both signs, and the main-paper figure reports weak Pearson correlations with firing rate, $\alpha$, and $\omega$ ($|r|\leq0.191$). This appendix treats those observations as evidence that projective reset directions remain diverse in the fast discharge state; it does not attach a universal biological meaning to either sign of $\lambda$.

\subsection{Feature-Perturbation Diagnostic}
\label{supp:sec:perturbation-diagnostic}

Figure~\ref{fig:supp_robustness_diagnostic} reports a fixed-checkpoint feature-space perturbation diagnostic on GSC and QTDB. Controlled input corruptions are widely used to probe non-adversarial robustness~\cite{hendrycks2019benchmarking}, while additive Gaussian jittering is a standard magnitude-domain transformation for time-series inputs~\cite{iwana2021empirical}. Motivated by these evaluation practices, we apply additive Gaussian noise, random feature zeroing, and their combination to saved SPRiF and ASRNN checkpoints, with matched perturbation settings within each condition. This diagnostic is not a replacement for the five-seed benchmark table in the main paper and is not used as a statistical robustness ranking; it records how the saved checkpoints respond under the specified feature perturbations.
