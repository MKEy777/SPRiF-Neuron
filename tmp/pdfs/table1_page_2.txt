We introduce SPRiF (Spectral Projective Reset Integrateand-Fire Neuron), a spiking neuron that addresses this question through functional state decomposition. SPRiF separates the neuron into two dynamically distinct components:
a slow spectral memory state and a fast discharge state.
The slow state integrates input through a constrained spectral structure—comprising a real exponential decay mode
and a two-dimensional damped rotation mode—providing
structured temporal filtering that extends beyond the singleexponential LIF memory trace. By design, the slow state is
never reset by spike events, preserving continuous temporal
memory across the spike train. The fast state receives projections from the slow state, produces the membrane readout,
generates binary spikes via threshold crossing, and absorbs
a novel projective reset along a learnable direction [1, λ]⊤
in its two-dimensional state space. SPRiF is fully compatible with standard SNN training: it uses backpropagation
through time (BPTT) with surrogate gradients, requiring no
specialized optimization. This design realizes what we term
the spike-never-resets-memory principle: spike events trigger corrections only on the fast discharge manifold, while
the slow spectral memory evolves continuously and undisturbed.
We emphasize that SPRiF’s functional decomposition is
qualitatively different from existing multi-timescale neuron
designs. Prior multi-timescale models assign different decay
rates or time constants to parallel processing channels—a
strategy we term rate decomposition—but all channels serve
the same dynamical role (memory accumulation and readout)
and are subject to the same reset. SPRiF instead assigns
different dynamical functions to its two states, enforcing the
separation structurally through the spectral constraint on the
slow state and the projective reset on the fast state, rather
than through heterogeneous time constants alone.
We evaluate SPRiF across five temporal processing
benchmarks spanning sequential image classification (SMNIST, PS-MNIST), physiological signal analysis (QTDB
ECG), speech recognition (Google Speech Commands), and
event-based digit recognition (Spiking Heidelberg Digits).
SPRiF achieves the highest accuracy in our comparison
on S-MNIST (99.28%), PS-MNIST (95.86%), and QTDB
(88.43%), and ranks second on GSC (94.55% vs. 94.84%)
and SHD (91.52% vs. 91.70%). Its parameter count is lower
than the strongest compared result on four datasets and comparable on QTDB. Mechanism ablations across PS-MNIST,
GSC, and QTDB isolate the contribution of rotation, state
separation, and projective reset. Complementary trajectory
and impulse-response analyses expose how these components alter the neuron’s internal dynamics rather than treating
SPRiF as a black-box replacement.
Contributions.
1. We propose functional state decomposition for spiking
neurons—separating temporal memory storage, spike
readout, and post-spike reset into dynamically distinct
state components, a principle that is orthogonal to existing rate-decomposition and single-state-reset approaches.

2. We introduce a constrained spectral slow state with real
exponential decay and damped rotation modes, providing interpretable, structured temporal filtering beyond the
single-exponential LIF trace.
3. We propose projective fast-state reset, a learnable directional reset mechanism that operates exclusively on the
discharge state and leaves the memory state untouched.
4. We provide empirical validation across five temporal
benchmarks, three mechanism ablations, and dynamical
analyses of state continuity and learned temporal kernels.

Related Work

Neuron Dynamics for Temporal Processing

Extensions of the leaky integrate-and-fire (LIF) neuron enrich temporal dynamics through gated integration, conductance, adaptive thresholds, or improved surrogate-gradient
training (Fang et al. 2022; Bai, Wang, and Yu 2026; Yao
et al. 2021; Huang et al. 2024a; Kamata et al. 2022). Adaptive and multi-timescale models further introduce slow variables, heterogeneous synaptic or neuronal decays, conductance channels, or learnable temporal gates (Baronig et al.
2025; Zhang et al. 2025b; Idrees et al. 2024; Wei et al. 2026;
Deng et al. 2025; Fulleda-Garcia, Soldado-Magraner, and
Margarit-Taulé 2026; Tiwari and Chauhan 2025). These approaches provide rate decomposition: parallel components
evolve at different time constants, but their roles remain coupled to membrane readout and reset.
Oscillatory and dendritic neurons offer complementary
ways to enrich temporal selectivity. Resonate-and-fire variants use damped oscillations (Izhikevich 2001; Higuchi et al.
2024; Huang et al. 2024b; Zhang et al. 2025a), whereas
compartmental or heterogeneous models introduce dendritic
structure and dynamics (Yin et al. 2024; Zheng et al. 2024;
Stevenson and Kappel 2023; Lange et al. 2021). Structured
state-space approaches have also been incorporated into spiking neurons (Fabre 2025; Shen et al. 2025). In contrast, SPRiF
uses functional decomposition: a constrained spectral slow
state retains continuous temporal memory, while a separate
fast state performs membrane readout and discharge. Thus,
the distinction is not only heterogeneous rates or spatial compartments, but structurally separated memory and spiking
functions.

2.2

Reset and Functional State Separation

Reset mechanisms have been softened, adapted, or removed
to reduce post-spike information loss or enable parallel training (Deng et al. 2022; Li et al. 2025; Huang et al. 2025;
Zhang 2025; Fang et al. 2023). However, these methods still
modify—or reason about removing the reset of—the state
that produces spikes. SPRiF instead confines a learnable
projective reset to the fast discharge state, leaving the slow
spectral state untouched; this preserves oscillatory phase and
temporal context across spike events. Network-level temporal
architectures, including temporal subnetworks and stochastic
spike communication (Ding et al. 2025b, 2024, 2025a), are
complementary to this neuron-level separation.
