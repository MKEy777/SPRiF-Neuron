4.2

Main Results

Table 1 shows that SPRiF attains the highest accuracy in
the comparison on S-MNIST, PS-MNIST, and QTDB. The
gain is small on S-MNIST (0.08 points) but increases to
0.50 points over the strongest TC-LIF configuration on PSMNIST and 1.43 points over BRF on QTDB. On GSC and
SHD, SPRiF trails the strongest reported result by 0.29 and
0.18 points, respectively. This pattern does not support a universal superiority claim; it shows that the same neuron mechanism remains competitive across pixel sequences, physiological signals, frame-based speech, and event streams.
The comparison is also parameter-efficient. SPRiF uses
0.067M parameters on S-MNIST and PS-MNIST, 1.77k on
QTDB, 0.13M on GSC, and 0.05M on SHD. Relative to
the strongest accuracy baseline in each column, this is approximately 55% fewer parameters on both MNIST variants,
comparable on QTDB (1.77k vs. 1.73k), 35% fewer on GSC,
and 55% fewer on SHD. Thus, the multi-state neuron does not
require a larger end-to-end network to achieve the reported
accuracy.

4.3

Mechanism Ablations

We isolate the three design choices on PS-MNIST, GSC, and
QTDB. Ablation A sets ω = 0, removing rotational coupling
while retaining the real decay path. Ablation B merges memory and discharge into a single three-dimensional state whose
first coordinate is the membrane and reset target. Ablation
C fixes λ = 0, reducing Eq. (8) to scalar reset. All variants
otherwise retain the task-specific training configuration.
Table 2 gives two consistent findings. First, merging the
states reduces accuracy by 2.38–5.04 points, directly supporting functional decomposition rather than state dimensionality alone. Second, rotation contributes 0.65–3.53 points,
with the largest effect on the long permuted sequence. Projective reset gives a smaller but consistent gain of 0.33–1.03
points. The ordering is informative: separating memory from
discharge is the primary mechanism, while spectral rotation
and a learnable reset direction refine how the two subsystems
process temporal structure.

4.4

Dynamical Analysis

The equations guarantee that reset does not directly modify
xt ; Figure 1 tests whether the intended separation is visible
in recorded and controlled dynamics. Around a spike from a
trained pSMNIST model, the three slow coordinates evolve
continuously while the fast membrane trace changes abruptly
(Fig. 1a). A separate cue–delay experiment shows that the
oscillatory slow state maintains a coherent phase trajectory
under controlled perturbation probes (Fig. 1b). The latter
record contains no binary spikes and is therefore used only as
phase-memory evidence, not as an event-level reset example.
Projective reset does not collapse to one shared direction.
Across QTDB, GSC, and pSMNIST, the learned λ values
include both signs (Fig. 1c–d). Their correlations with firing
rate, α, and ω are weak in these models, with |r| ≤ 0.191
(Fig. 1e). Together with the consistent λ = 0 ablation loss,
these observations support learned directional diversity in the
fast state. They do not assign a universal biological meaning
to either sign.
We next inject a unit impulse into trained neurons and
record 100 steps of intrinsic slow-state evolution. Alphastratified examples span rapid and persistent real traces
while the oscillatory coordinate changes sign and frequency
(Fig. 2a). All-neuron spectra further show broad within-task
variation in effective low-pass behavior (Fig. 2b). Thus the
constrained spectral state is used to form heterogeneous temporal kernels rather than collapsing to one exponential response.
For the slow-decay examples in Fig. 2c, the SPRiF real
modes have derived time constants of approximately 272
steps on GSC and 138 on QTDB, whereas the closest available trained ASRNN membrane constants are 34 and 46
steps. This comparison is intentionally restricted to GSC and
QTDB, the two tasks for which trained ASRNN kernels are
present in the saved analysis data. It isolates a long-memory
regime but does not establish that every SPRiF neuron is
longer-lived than every adaptive-LIF neuron.

5

Limitations

SPRiF trades one scalar state for five state values per neuron,
increasing activation memory and neuron-local arithmetic
even when the end-to-end parameter count is small. The reported benchmark numbers are single runs and several baselines are taken from their original papers under task-specific
protocols; repeated-seed statistics and a unified reimplementation would be required for significance claims. The current
evaluation focuses on small- to medium-scale temporal classification and does not establish energy gains on neuromorphic hardware. Finally, the diagnostic analyses demonstrate
state continuity and diverse kernels, but do not prove that every learned mode is necessary for every input. These boundaries motivate repeated-seed evaluation, matched wall-clock
and spike-operation measurements, and larger long-context
benchmarks.
